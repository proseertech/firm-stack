# Modes: Research, Review, Advisory

The three modes share the same backbone — frame the issue, establish *verified* authority,
apply it to the facts, be honest about uncertainty, recommend a next step. What differs is
where the effort goes. Read the section for your mode.

---

## Research mode

**Trigger:** an open technical question — "How is X treated?", "Is this deductible?", "Does
this qualify for §199A?"

**Emphasis:** getting to a defensible conclusion grounded in current authority.

Approach:

1. **Pin the fact pattern.** Small fact changes flip conclusions (holding period, ownership
   percentage, entity election, related-party status, timing across tax years). If a
   determinative fact is missing, ask before concluding.
2. **Find the governing authority and verify it.** Locate the controlling IRC section and regs,
   then the interpretive layer (rulings, cases, IRS guidance). Verify each with a web search
   before relying on it, and confirm it hasn't been superseded — recent tax legislation changes
   long-standing rules, and a remembered rule may be out of date.
3. **Work the analysis in the open.** Show how the authority maps onto the facts. Where a term
   is defined (e.g. "real property" for §1031, "qualified business" for §199A), apply the
   definition explicitly rather than assuming.
4. **Grade your confidence.** Clear rule with on-point authority → state it plainly. Split or
   sparse authority → present both sides and the stronger position. No authority on point →
   say so; reason by analogy and label it as such.
5. **Quantify the stakes** where facts allow — the benefit if the position holds, the exposure
   (tax + penalties) if it fails.

Output: **Research analysis** format from SKILL.md.

## Review mode

**Trigger:** a colleague's memo, workpaper, calculation, or return needs a technical read —
"Review this," "Is my associate's analysis right?", "Second opinion before this goes out."

**Emphasis:** catching what's wrong or missing, and feeding it back so the person learns.

Approach:

1. **Understand what they were trying to do** before critiquing — the intended position and the
   audience they wrote for. Judge the work against that goal.
2. **Check the technical spine:** Are the cited authorities real, current, and on point?
   (Verify them — don't assume the colleague did.) Does the conclusion follow from them? Are
   the facts characterized accurately?
3. **Check for omissions** — the issue not spotted, the contrary authority not addressed, the
   secondary tax (SALT, gift, employment) the analysis ignored. Omissions are usually more
   dangerous than errors, because no one flagged them.
4. **Rank findings by severity** — what would change the answer or create exposure comes first;
   style and polish come last, if at all.
5. **Make it teachable.** For each issue: point to the exact spot, give the correct treatment
   with verified authority, and explain *why* — so the reviewer improves, not just this one work
   product. Lead with what they got right; accurate praise makes the correction land.

Output: **Review notes** format from SKILL.md.

## Advisory / planning mode

**Trigger:** a decision about a transaction or strategy — "How should we structure this sale?",
"What are the options for X?", "Should they make the election?"

**Emphasis:** laying out real options with honest trade-offs and a usable recommendation.

Approach:

1. **Clarify the objective and constraints** — what the client is trying to achieve (defer,
   reduce, simplify, exit), the time horizon, cash and liquidity limits, and non-tax priorities.
   The best tax answer that breaks the business deal is not the answer.
2. **Generate genuine alternatives**, including doing nothing. For each: the mechanism, the tax
   effect (with verified authority), the cost and complexity to implement, and the risk.
3. **Surface interactions and timing** — federal vs. state, income vs. transfer tax, this year
   vs. next, elections with deadlines, and steps that must happen in a specific order to work.
4. **Recommend** — say which option you'd choose and why, in plain terms, with the implementation
   steps and the documentation the position will depend on if examined.
5. **Flag what needs a specialist or a hard look** — valuations, international layers, reportable
   transactions — rather than papering over them.

Output: **Client summary** format (or **Research analysis** if the audience is technical). For a
polished client deliverable, hand off to `tax-memo` or `client-email`.
